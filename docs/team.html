01

The shift

If the fleet writes the code, what is left for the people?

Boris Cherny, who created Claude Code, says the old job functions — engineering, product, design, data science — are blurring into one kind of role. On his own team he sees five recurring patterns:

Prototyper· Builder· Sweeper· Grower· Maintainer
The delegation gap

At Anthropic — the most AI-forward team measured — engineers use AI in about 60% of their work, but can fully hand a task to it only 0–20% of the time. The judgment stays with people. These roles are about judgment, not typing.

The review economics

If a person reviews every AI change at normal speed, the whole speed-up is eaten by review — data on 10,000+ developers shows review time up 91% and changes up 154%. So review can’t cover everything; it has to be tiered, with people looking only at what matters.

Both point the same way: the human’s job moves from writing the change to deciding what may pass. That is why the fleet handles routine review itself and saves people for the gates that carry real risk (◆).
02

The team

Take one hard case: rebuilding a bank’s payment switch from scratch. The old roles collapse into five seats — six to eight people, each owning one clear part of the system. (Regulation asks for named sign-offs and a few strict separations, not for particular job titles.)

SeatOwnsIts workArchetypeWhat the role does
Product architect intentwhat should exist the build plan · the contracts · acceptance criteria Builder + Grower Owns what the system should do, and the contracts between its parts. Proposes each sign-off but never signs off their own work; settles the questions the machines escalate; builds prototypes directly with agents instead of writing specs for others.
Domain orchestrators deliverywhat gets built working, reviewed slices — authorization · clearing & settlement · connectors Builder A domain expert who scales through agents, not a generalist juggling tasks. Runs several feature builds at once in one area, steers the specs, and judges the hard cases — the domain details no automated check catches: reversals, timeouts, stand-in processing.
Harness engineer controlwhat may pass the gates, checks, and the shadow-diff engine Maintainer + Sweeper Builds the gates and checks as real, tested code — the test rules, requirement-to-test tracing, locked test suites, the parallel-run diff. Owns the CI side of DevOps: the build pipeline is where the gates actually run. An auditor’s questions land on this seat’s code.
Reliability engineer runtimewhat keeps running environments · monitoring · the traffic mirror · the replay records Maintainer Needed from day one, not just at launch: the environments, the secure HSM links, the shadow copy that runs live traffic, and safe-keeping of the replay records. Owns the CD side of DevOps: deployment, infrastructure-as-code, the machines the pipeline runs on. Becomes the classic keep-it-running role at go-live.
Trust engineer trusttwo pairs of hands keys · PCI scope · the adversarial pass on money & PII Compliance Runs the key ceremonies — a two-person, in-the-room step no automation may touch — plus secrets, code signing, and deploy credentials. Regulation forces this seat; the standard tech role list has no name for it, which is telling.
Two hard rules: nobody approves their own work, and nobody runs what they can change. The seat that decides what reaches production can’t touch production; the seat that runs production can’t change the gates.
